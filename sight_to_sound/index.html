Sight to Sound

Sep 2016 - May 2017

What's this?

A combination of glasses, a camera and earbuds (the camera is the small hole on the nose bridge).
It takes live feed video footage, converts it into sound, and plays it into the earbuds.

Convert images to sound?

This "sensory substitution" was the key focus of this project. The main approach is explained in the video below.

Originally developed in Java in Eclipse IDE.

This project was presented at the 2017 Long Island Math Fair, where a gold medal was awarded.
An academic paper was also written on the project, which can be read here.

The Sight to Sound Game

Objective: Move the yellow circle to the right side of the screen. However, you are not allowed to look.
Louder sounds mean that the obstacles are closer. Sounds are localized between left and right ear.

Purpose: To test to see if people can actually use sound to map their surroundings.

U: Turn sound on   I: Turn sound off   WASD: Move   Ctrl+R: Refresh map

You must keep your eyes closed to play! Headphones are reccomended.

It turns out to be quite difficult! Most participations lost their sense of direction rather quickly.
The conclusion from this test is that the process of coverting images to sound is much more involved
that a simple distance-volume relationship.

The Problem

In this demo, we have just one sound (white noise) being played at different volumes.
While it might be a good distance cue, traversing an area requires learning about the space.

When we see objects, our eyes are being bombarded with photons from all directions.
Our brain knows how to group these into objects.

We do not have this effect for our hearing! The way our brain can pick out different instruments in an orchestra
is a much more complex decomposition process than hard cutoffs at each frequency/voume/source location.
This obstacle would need to be overcome before a successful prototype could be realized.
